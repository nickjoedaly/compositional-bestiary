## The Ache of Distance

If SEAWATER: I explored the fluid dissolution of personhood in water, SEAWATER: II turns inward to the emotional texture of yearning—that particular ache that arises when desire meets distance, when the hoped-for remains just beyond reach. Yearning is not satisfaction nor pure hunger; it is the sustained condition of reaching toward something only partially articulated, a fundamental human orientation toward incompleteness.

In this movement, the ensemble becomes a chorus of individual yearnings. High violins and flutes state fragmented melodies that never quite resolve, interrupted by deeper voices that seem to question and echo. Harmonic motion becomes unpredictable, with sudden shifts in color and register that suggest the disorientation of desire. Rhythmic coordination fragments—while one section maintains a steady pulse, others accelerate or drift into rubato, creating a temporal landscape of asynchronous longing. The instrumentation thickens gradually, moving from sparse and transparent textures toward something more immersive, suggesting both the accumulation of feeling and the loss of individual identity within a collective emotion. By the movement's close, the ensemble hovers in harmonic ambiguity, unresolved and breathing—not tragedy, but the bittersweet knowledge that yearning itself may be the truest music we have.
