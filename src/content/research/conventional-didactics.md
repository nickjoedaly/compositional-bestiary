## Surveying the Field

Conventional composition pedagogy has long operated within institutional frameworks that privilege certain forms of knowledge while marginalizing others. The standard curriculum‚Äîbuilt around four-part harmony, functional tonality, and formal analysis‚Äîassumes a linear progression from rule-based mastery to creative expression. Yet this assumption obscures the blind spots inherent in such pedagogy: the ways notation becomes a gatekeeper, the unexamined biases toward Western European harmonic traditions, and the systematic erasure of autodidactic and cross-tradition learning paths.

Contemporary composition teachers inherit a pedagogical apparatus designed for specific institutional contexts‚Äîthe conservatory, the universityÎäThat privileges those with access to notation literacy and staff paper. This inheritance renders composition pedagogy fundamentally under-theorized as a *philosophy* of knowledge-making. We teach technsques but rarely interrogate why these techniques matter, what assumptions they encode, or how they might exclude those who learn differently: the musician from an oral tradition, the self-taught composer working through sound rather than symbol, the practitioner bridging disparate musical cosmologies.

## The Failure to Serve the Autodidact

The pedagogical crisis deepens when we consider how thoroughly conventional didactics fails the autodidact and the cross-tradition musician. Notation-dependent instruction creates a barrier that is simultaneously symbolic and material: it requires particular tools, particular literacies, particular access. Where is the pedagogy that honors learning through ear training conducted in the idiom of one's own tradition? Where is the theory that accounts for the composer who moves between Turkish makam and minimalist repetition, between Quranic intonation and algorithmic composition? Conventional pedagogy offers fragmented answers, treating such crossings as exotic exceptions rather than central pedagogical concerns. To reimagine composition didactics is thus to ask: what would pedagogy look like if it were designed not for the conservatory but for the *thinking practitioner*‚Äîwhoever and wherever they might be?
