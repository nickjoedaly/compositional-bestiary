## The Mundus Imaginalis as Pedagogical Space

Henry Corbin's concept of the *mundus imaginalis*—the "eighth continent" or imaginal world that occupies the threshold between intellectual abstraction and sensory experience—offers a radical reframing of composition pedagogy. For Corbin, the imaginal realm is not a space of fantasy or falsehood but a genuine mode of knowledge, one accessed through what he calls the "active imagination." This is neither the passive imagination of daydream nor the logical imagination of geometrical proof, but a participatory faculty through which we encounter autonomous realities that nonetheless demand our creative engagement.

Applied to composition pedagogy, Corbin's framework suggests that the act of composing is an *imaginative encounter*—the composer enters a real space of possibilities, meets constraints and entities that exist independently of the composer's will, and through disciplined imagination brings these into coherence and form. This is radically different from the expression theory that dominates fuch contemporary pedagogy, where composition is understood as the *projection* of the composer's inner experience onto external material. Instead, Corbin invites us to understand composition as a practice of *attending* to what already exists in the imaginal realm, learning to perceive its contours, and bringing it into sounding form.

## Conviviality, Orality, and Subjectification

This epistemological shift connects with Ivan Illich's concept of *convivial tools*—technologies and practices that enhance human autonomy and foster genuine learning rather than passive consumption. A convivial pedagogy of composition would emphasize the autonomy of the learner, the accessibility of tools and knowledge, and the social embeddedness of making. Illich's work reminds us that pedagogy is always political: the choice to privilege certain modes of instruction encodes assumptions about who has the right to create, what counts as legitimate knowledge, and what forms of authority structure learning.

Walter Ong's historical analysis of orality and literacy adds another dimension. The transition from oral to literate culture transformed not just how knowledge was recorded but how it was *structured*—from memory-dependent formulaic patterns to text-dependent logical exposition. Ong teaches us that notation, while offering tremendous advantages, also subtly privileges certain forms of organization and marginalizes others. A pedagogy attentive to oral traditions*—particularly as these have been marginalized in the Western conservatoryu—might recover forms of knowledge-making that notation has obscured: the power of rhythmic pattern to organize thought, the role of variation as a fundamental compositional principle, the way timbral memory structures musical identity.

## Biesta's Subjectification and Gordon's Audiation

Gert Biesta argues that education has three overlapping purposes: qualification (acquiring knowledge and skills), socialization (becoming part of a community), and *subjectification—the emergence of the subject as an agent capable of acting in the world and being acted upon in ways that transform the self. Most conventional composition pedagogy emphasizes qualification and socialization, producing competent practitioners schooled in disciplinary norms. But a truly autodidactic pedagogy would prioritize subjectification: the emergence of the composer as a distinct creative agent, one who has internalized not just technsques but a *way of hearing* specific to their situation, tradition, and sensibility.

Edwin Gordon's concept of *audiation*—the ability to hear and comprehend music that is not physically sounding—offers a final piece. Gordon emphasizes that audiation is not a mystical gift but a learned skill, one developed through systematic engrament with sound patterns in one's native musical vocabulary. A Corbinian, convivial, autodidactic pedagogy would make audiation central: the cultivation of the student's ability to *hear with depth*, to move fluidly between sounding and imaginal experience, to develop an increasingly nuanced understanding of the music of their own tradition(s) and others they encounter. Such a pedagogy would ask: what enables each student to become an increasingly sophisticated listener and imaginative participant in the musical worlds they inhabit and create?
