Annotated Bibliography

Three Strands

The bibliography organized by intellectual lineage.

STRAND I

Ecomusicology & Deep Listening

Sound as ecological practice. Attention to the sounding world as the foundation of all compositional work.

2010   Abram, David. Becoming Animal: An Earthly Cosmology. New York: Vintage. — Extends the ecophenomenology of The Spell of the Sensuous into a theory of reciprocal perception between human and more-than-human worlds.
1996   Abram, David. The Spell of the Sensuous: Perception and Language in a More-than-Human World. New York: Vintage. — The foundational text of ecophenomenology. Argues that alphabetic literacy restructured human perception away from animate nature.
2005   Oliveros, Pauline. Deep Listening: A Composer's Sound Practice. Lincoln, NE: iUniverse. — Listening as contemplative, compositional, and communal practice. The methodological ancestor of the Bestiary's “inner tuition.”
1977   Schafer, R. Murray. The Soundscape: Our Sonic Environment and the Tuning of the World. Rochester, VT: Destiny Books. — Soundscape ecology as music pedagogy. The ear-cleaning exercises anticipate the Bestiary's attention-training creatures.
1974   Schafer, R. Murray. The New Soundscape. Toronto: Berandol Music. — Educational companion to the soundscape project. Exercises for reshaping auditory awareness.
· · ·
STRAND II

Art / Technology Dialectics

The pharmakon question: how tools shape, constrain, and enable creative consciousness.

2010   Stiegler, Bernard. Taking Care of Youth and the Generations. Trans. Stephen Barker. Stanford: Stanford UP. — Technology as pharmakon; the battle for attention as the central pedagogical crisis of the digital age.
1998   Stiegler, Bernard. Technics and Time, 1: The Fault of Epimetheus. Trans. Richard Beardsworth and George Collins. Stanford: Stanford UP. — The human is constituted through its technical prostheses. Tools are not external to thought; they are its condition.
1972   Illich, Ivan. Tools for Conviviality. New York: Harper & Row. — The Illichian criterion: does the tool expand or contract the user's autonomy? The Bestiary's design test.
1982   Ong, Walter J. Orality and Literacy: The Technologizing of the Word. London: Methuen. — How writing systems restructure consciousness. Applied here to musical notation as a technology of thought.
1650   Kircher, Athanasius. Musurgia Universalis. Rome. — Contains the Arca Musarithmica. The earliest systematic algorithmic composition tool and the Bestiary's most direct ancestor.
1972   Eno, Brian. Oblique Strategies (with Peter Schmidt). — Constraint cards as creative partners. Generative systems as tools for overcoming habitual thought. A key exemplar for the Bestiary's creature-as-encounter model.
· · ·
STRAND III

Practice-Based Research & Education Philosophy

Methodological grounding and the theory of what education is for.

2012   Borgdorff, Henk. The Conflict of the Faculties: Perspectives on Artistic Research and Academia. Leiden: Leiden UP. — Theoretical foundation of artistic research as a legitimate mode of knowledge production.
1993   Frayling, Christopher. “Research in Art and Design.” Royal College of Art Research Papers 1:1. — The seminal distinction between research into, through, and for art and design.
2014   Biesta, Gert. The Beautiful Risk of Education. Boulder: Paradigm. — Education as the creation of conditions for subjectification—the emergence of the student as a unique person. The philosophical ground for the Bestiary's “personhood as sacred” commitment.
1972   Corbin, Henry. Mundus Imaginalis, or the Imaginary and the Imaginal. Spring. — The essay that names the intermediate realm. Applied here as the ontological ground for the Bestiary's creatures—beings that are neither fantasy nor fact.
1975   Bakhtin, Mikhail. “Forms of Time and of the Chronotope in the Novel.” In The Dialogic Imagination. Austin: University of Texas Press. — Time-space as inseparable. Each Bestiary creature as a chronotope: a world with its own temporal and spatial physics.

This bibliography is a living document and will grow throughout the doctoral process.
