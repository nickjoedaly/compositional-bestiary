Theoretical Framework

The Philosophical Constellation

Eight thinkers whose work shapes the Bestiary's epistemological architecture.

The Compositional Bestiary draws on a philosophical constellation that spans phenomenology, theology, media theory, education philosophy, and ecocriticism. These thinkers are not cited as authorities to be deferred to, but as interlocutors whose ideas become operational through the Bestiary's creative practice. Each one illuminates a different facet of the central question: What happens when a person encounters a structured compositional system, and what kind of knowing does that encounter produce?

  • Mundus Imaginalis Henry Corbin (1903–1978). The French philosopher and Islamicist described a mundus imaginalis—a real-but-not-physical realm accessed through active imagination, neither pure fantasy nor empirical fact. The Bestiary's creatures inhabit this intermediate world: they are imaginal beings that become real through the student's encounter with them. Composition as Corbinian visitation. The Bestiary is a cartography of this imaginal territory.
  • Pharmakon Bernard Stiegler (1952–2020). Technology is simultaneously poison and cure—pharmakon. AI composition tools can replace human creativity or train it, depending entirely on design. Stiegler's framework provides the critical lens: the Bestiary is an argument for the curative orientation of AI, for technology as care rather than capture.
  • Subjectification Gert Biesta (b. 1957). The Dutch education philosopher distinguishes three functions of education: qualification (skills), socialization (norms), and subjectification (the emergence of the person as a unique subject). The Bestiary is primarily concerned with the third: not making competent technicians but enabling the emergence of irreducible musical selves.
  • Convivial Tools Ivan Illich (1926–2002). Tools should serve personal rather than institutional ends. A convivial technology expands human autonomy rather than creating new dependencies. The Bestiary is designed as an Illichian tool: it increases the student's capacity for independent compositional thought rather than binding them to a platform or a pedagogue.
  • Orality / Literacy Walter Ong (1912–2003). Writing restructures consciousness. Musical notation is not neutral—it reconfigures musical thought, enabling certain kinds of complexity while foreclosing others. The Bestiary operates in the productive tension between oral and literate musicianship, using notation as a bridge rather than a gate.
  • Ecophenomenology David Abram (b. 1957). The philosopher of perception argues that sensory awareness is ecological: attention to sound is attention to place, body, and the animate earth. Deep listening (Oliveros) becomes an ecological practice. The Bestiary's “inner tuition” is also an outer attunement—a re-sensitization to the sounding world.
  • Chronotope Mikhail Bakhtin (1895–1975). The inseparability of time and space in narrative. Each creature in the Bestiary creates a distinct compositional chronotope—a specific configuration of temporal and spatial relationships that shapes what music can emerge within it. To enter a creature is to enter a world with its own physics.
  • Artistic Research Christopher Frayling / Henk Borgdorff. Practice-based methodology where the creative work is the research. The Bestiary's compositions, plugins, and pedagogical encounters are not illustrations of a theory—they are the theory's primary evidence. Research through art, not merely about it.

SUPPLEMENTARY

Adjacent Thinkers

Beyond the core eight, the Bestiary engages with:

  • Edwin Gordon — Audiation theory as the secular-cognitive parallel to Corbin's active imagination. Hearing music before it sounds.
  • Pauline Oliveros — Deep Listening as contemplative compositional practice.
  • R. Murray Schafer — Soundscape ecology and ear cleaning as foundational pedagogy.
  • Rebecca Solnit — The atlas-as-narrative form (Unfathomable City) as model for the Bestiary's eventual book form.
  • Ben Johnston — Model of the long-term, independent compositional career.
  • Harry Partch — Just intonation as philosophical stance; the chronotope of American tuning.
